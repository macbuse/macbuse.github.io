Squeezing and differentiability

We want to show that the euclidean norm ball is smooth in a round about way by viewing it as the graph over a line segment and then bounding the graph from above by the graph of a very simple quadratic function. This method works in general provided that the point in question can be approximated from the right and left by points corresponding to primitive homology classes.

Approximation The notion of approximated from the right and left was introduced in my thesis for geodesics on a punctured surface but for the torus it is just a fact about continued fractions (see here : if ζ is an irrational and pn/qn the sequence of convergents then (after possibly renumbering) p2n/q2n < ζ < p2n + 1/q2n + 1.

You should think of the vectors below as being v0 = (p2n,q2n), v1 = (p2n + 1,q2n + 1).

Let v0, v1 ∈ ℝ2 be pair of linearly independent vectors and ∥.∥ be an arbitrary norm on ℝ.

Given a norm ∥.∥ then there are two obvious norms on ℝ that one can compare it with:

  1. The one norm ∥xv0 + yv1∥1 := |x|∥v0∥ + |v|∥v1∥
  2. The hilbert norm ∥xv0 + yv1∥22 := |x|2∥v0∥2 + 2∥v0∥∥v1∥cos (θ) + |y|2∥v1∥2

Here θ might be the angle between v0, v1 but it may be thought of as an arbitrary parameter.

By a standard calculation

∥xv0 + yv1∥12 − ∥xv0 + yv1∥22 = 2|x||y|∥v0∥∥v1∥sin2(θ/2)

which gives

∥xv0 + yv1∥1 − ∥xv0 + yv1∥2 = (2|x||y|∥v0∥∥v1∥sin2(θ/2))/(xv0+yv1∥1+∥xv0+yv1∥2)

so that

0 ≤ ∥xv0 + yv1∥1 − ∥xv0 + yv1∥2 ≤ (2|x||y|∥v0∥∥v1∥sin2(θ/2))/(2∥xv0+yv1∥2).

Now we want to show that the euclidean norm ball is smooth in a round about way. We divide by norms and suppose that ∥v0∥ = ∥v1∥ = 1 and define vt := (1−t)v0 + tv1 so that the estimate above becomes: 0 ≤ ∥vt∥1 − ∥vt∥2 ≤ 2t(1−t)sin2(θ/2)/m, where m := inf0 ≤ t ≤ 1∥vt∥2, which is bounded away from 0 since v0, v1 is a basis and under the hypothesis ∥v0∥ = ∥v1∥ = 1 it is just cos (θ/2). Now to simplify the argument we will suppose that vt is a horizontal line and we consider the portion of the euclidean norm ball above the segment vt, 0 ≤ t ≤ 1 it is easy to see that this is the graph of a function which lies between the line segment and the graph of t ↦ 2t(1−t)sin2(θ/2)/m, which is a parabola. Now we are interested in the behavior of vectors tangent to support planes as θ → 0. The distance between v0 and v1 is O(θ) and the distance between v0 and vt is roughly t and we’ll use this later to calculate tangents.

For the point A(t) on the parabola corresponding to t the tangent vector is inside a cone bounded by lines tangent to A(0)A(t) and A(1)A(t). One can estimate the slope of these lines in: in the case of A(0)A(t) this is

(2t(1−t)sin2(θ/2))/(2tsin(θ/2)) = (1−t)sin (θ/2).

So this is bounded between 0 and sin (θ/2).

By elementary geometry the same estimate is holds for points on the portion of the euclidean norm ball squeezed between the chord (the ∥.∥1 norm ball) and the parabola. It is easy to see from this that the norm ball is differentiable.

Comparison norm

The above argument will work for the stable norm ∥.∥s provided we can bound it from above (locally) by a euclidean norm. This follows from a general principle in negative curvature: distances are always greater than they are in a “comparable” euclidean/flat configuration.

Let v0 and v1 be primitive homology classes which form a basis. Then one has: sinh (∥v0∥s/2)sinh (∥v1∥s/2)sin (θh) = 1, where θh is the angle between the corresponding simple closed geodesics at the unique point where they intersect (see this article). Because of the sinh  factors the angle between hyperbolic geodesics tends to zero exponentially faster than for the comparable geodesics on a euclidean torus. This is a manifestation of exponential divergence of Jacobi fields.

From this data, that is the angle θh, we can construct a comparison norm ∥xv0 + yv1∥e2 := |x|2∥v0∥2 + 2∥v0∥∥v1∥cos (θh) + |y|2∥v1∥2.

Theorem [CAT+] The restrictions of the stable norm and the comparison norms to the the positive cone {xv0 + yv1, x, y ∈ ℝ+}. satisfy

∥v∥e ≤ ∥v∥s ≤ ∥v∥1.

This follows from: - an inductive enumeration of primitive homology classes (with positive coefficients) which is really due to Farey. - Toponogov’s Theorem
